Understanding climate change with data

Climate change is one of the hardest pressing issues that modern society faces. From the advent of transport automation via coal, the environment that we know is slowly becoming perilous. This coupled with the fact that corporations are diminishing and ruining natural resources on our planet; there is an influx of smog, CO2 (Carbon dioxide) emissions, and so much more other harmful pollutant in the ozone of the Earth. Climate change is real and is already having a significant impact on our ecosystems, economies, and our communities.

In a nutshell, climate change is defined by NASA as the variations in the average conditions, from temperatures to rain falls, over a long period of time. On the other hand, weather is described as the ecological conditions of a particular place, such as it’s hot or raining in Washington DC. Why studying the deviations of climate change is important, because of the simple fact that we only have one Earth, and that isn’t going to change for a while.

Studying climate change will give us an understanding of why the temperatures are rising and worsened high and low extreme weather patterns to understanding why wildfires are more readily apparent then now then before. Even though the average temperatures see a rise in half a degree over the past 50 years, this massive and colossal effects on our ecosystem. We as human beings have a right to protect what gave 100 billion people (yes you read that correctly) food, warmth, and shelter. It’s upon us to act and act when it matters the most.

Global Warming

As we know that climate change is understanding the variations in weather patterns over a long period of time, global warming, on the other hand, is taking a look into how these temperatures are changing due to human activities. These activities include burning of fossil fuels, deforestation, dirtier air, and an increase in acidic oceans. An amalgamation of all these different processes cause greenhouse gasses, such as carbon dioxide and methane, to be trapped in the environment and slowly deplete the ozone layer.

Years ago, the human race didn’t have the technologies to cause global scale effects on the Earth as we do today. However, with modern technology such as cars and trucks/vessels, we’re increasing the amount of harmful pollutants in the air at a level which nature can’t combat. This, as a result, causes drastic deviations in climate change.
